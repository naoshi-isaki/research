<p>This is a great list of books on nationalism - with one exception. A brief thread on why Imagined Communities is one of the most overrated books in the field - and what other books should replace it on any reading list on the subject. 1/</p>
<p>In my graduate seminar this week we focus on nations and national states.Â </p>
<p>In addition to rereading three great works from the 1980s (top row), I am engaging with three post-2000 books by the macrosociologist Andreas Wimmer (bottom row).</p>
<p>First, on what I mean by overrated - one way to measure a book's position in the field is its citation count. Eight years ago I compiled a ranking of the most cited books in social science, as measured by Google Scholar (https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/impactofsocialsciences/2016/05/12/what-are-the-most-cited-publications-in-the-social-sciences-according-to-google-scholar/). IC ranked 5th on the list. 2/</p>
<p>This puts it above any book by Bourdieu, Foucault, Marx or Rawls, among many others. But how is it being cited? I don't have data on this but I would hazard ~99% of citations merely refer to the book's title, which is that nations can be described as Imagined Communities. 3/</p>
<p>But very few citations of IC seem to actually engage with the book's real argument, which is that the origins of modern nationalism lie in the development of print capitalism, in particular the rise of newspapers (or â€œone-day best sellersâ€ as Anderson calls them). 4/</p>
<p>It is newspapers and novels that allowed people to think of themselves as members of Imagined Communities - along with the role of colonial administrative pilgrimages and what Anderson calls "official nationalism." This is the basic argument of the book. 5/</p>
<p>Yet IC's actual argument has drawn vanishingly little critical attention: as White (2004) notes, â€œrarely has a critical best-seller been so popular and so ignored at the same timeâ€ [https://jstor.org/stable/pdf/40068215.pdf], which still remains the case 20 years later. 6/</p>
<p>One partial piece of support for one part of IC's argument comes from Sasaki (2017), who shows that the spread of the printing press is correlated with the publication of dictionaries and language standardization in Europe. [https://cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-economic-history/article/publishing-nations-technology-acquisition-and-language-standardization-for-european-ethnic-groups/64CC9AFDA14AA5177455996B70382307] 7/</p>
<p>But largely scholarship has found very mixed evidence for the role of print capitalism. In a 2016 symposium I showed that media coverage in Africa is correlated with less, not more, national identification, or the opposite of what IC would predict [https://eprints.lse.ac.uk/67407/1/Imagined%20communities_2016.pdf]. 8/</p>
<p>More recently Pengl et al. (2022) showed that print technology in modern Africa is correlated with greater attachment to ethnic over national identities: again, the opposite of IC's argument. [https://cambridge.org/core/journals/american-political-science-review/article/cash-crops-print-technologies-and-the-politicization-of-ethnicity-in-africa/847987F47DD0BAB3BE5EC01AF780DE24] 9/</p>
<p>Perhaps part of the problem is IC's over-reliance upon evidence from SE Asia relative to other regions. Anecdotally, I have found those who have the biggest respect for IC to be SE Asian specialists, so my claim for being overrated might lie in its applicability elsewhere. 10/</p>
<p>In any case I remain unconvinced IC deserves its place among the pantheon of nationalism studies. Other books that really deserve to be read more than IC include Rogers Brubaker's Nationalism Reframed (1996), Liah Greenfeld's Nationalism: Five Roads to Modernity (1992), ... 11/</p>
<p>... David Laitin's Nations, States and Violence (2007), Philip Roeder's Where Nation-States Come From (2007), Anthony Smith's Ethnic Origins of Nations (1986) and, most recently,Â <br />@MayaJTudor<br />Â andÂ <br />@hmylonas<br />'s book Varieties of Nationalism (2023). This is only a partial list! 12/</p>
<p>My ultimate point in this thread is not to criticize IC as a bad book per se but to point out that there are high opportunity costs to assigning texts to students in such a crowded field, and we need to get students engaging with the best literature on the subject. 13/13 End</p>
