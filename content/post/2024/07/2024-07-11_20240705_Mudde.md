<p>Seeing a lot of poor takes on #UKElection2024 already, so a quick (completely unasked for) #thread ğŸ§µ before I get my heart broken in the #GERSPA game.</p>
<p>1. First and foremost, this is a HORRIBLE electoral system!!! We might be happy now, because of the outcome, but this is the type of wildly disproportionate system that brought OrbÃ¡n to a super majority and everything that has happened since.</p>
<p><img src="https://pbs.twimg.com/media/GRu-kjEbMAAIfVH?format=jpg&amp;name=large" alt="ç”»åƒ" width="500" height="322" /></p>
<p>2. Labour has just 10% more votes and 3.5 times more seats?!?!</p>
<p>Not to speak of Reform Party, which is almost as big as AfD but has just 4 seats.</p>
<p>3. Labour has a mandate because it has the seats, that is how the system works -- and worked before for Tories. It is a supermajority and means Starmer can pretty much do what he wants.</p>
<p>4. Just because Reform Party won big, doesn't mean immigration is the key issue of "the people". In many ways, Reform represents a disappointment of nativist voters with Tories and an obsessive focus on immigration by Tories and media (weakly supported by Labour).</p>
<p>5. In other words, immigration wasn't more important then before, nor is it a "spontaneous" issue. It can be made big and it can be made smaller. And it can lead to votes for Tories or for Reform.</p>
<p>6. Hence, there is NO reason for Starmer to center immigration and push for a nativist (lite) course. He has four years to change policies and perceptions as well as lower the salience of immigration (as had happened after Brexit).</p>
<p>7. I still hold some (naive) hope that Starmer will govern more left-wing than he said in his campaign -- just as Biden has in the US. It is not "betrayal", as many progressive voters voted for Labour too.</p>
<p>8. There are virtually NO general lessons to learn from this election. It was highly specific. One party had been in power for almost 15 years, pushing through unique experience of Brexit, going through unprecedented # of leaders/PMs, and scandal after scandal.</p>
<p>9. In this light, 33% of the votes (and just 20% of eligible voters, given 60% turnout), is pretty poor. Also, given the electoral system, which gives Labour tactical advantage over social liberals and greens, whom would be stronger competitors in proportional systems.</p>
<p>10. The idea that Starmer proves that a "moderate" course pays off, is therefore nonsense. There is little reason to assume an openly progressive course (including strong position on Gaza) would not have provided a huge majority.</p>
<p>11. And, again, and this is CRUCIAL, the dynamics in a two-party system are fundamentally different from those in a majoritarian system. Hence, lessons are ALWAYS very problematic.</p>
<p>12. This is all not to piss on Labour's parade or to deny the really important victory for British democracy -- and for marginalized groups and people in the UK. The Tories were awful for democracy, minority rights, the welfare state, etc.</p>
<p>13. But it is fairly basic victory, achieved with an overly cautious and bland campaign, that mostly catered to the right. The super majority is there to truly make difference. I hope Starmer ignores the media, pundits, and party whisperers, and DARES to lead rather than follow.</p>
<p>14. And I hope that progressive parties on "the Continent" do not think they can copy the results of Labour, with whatever campaign, as the systemic differences are just too big. #TheEnd</p>
</blockquote>
