<p>The Tradition in Context 1/2. Critical juncture research is a tradition that was launched by Seymour Lipset and Stein Rokkan in 1967. It is part of a large intellectual movement that embraces historical analysis and seeks to go beyond a focus on short-term causal relations.</p>
<p>The Tradition in Context 2/2. CJ research shares interests with comparative historical analysis, historical institutionalism, the new institutional economics, American Political Development, and historical political economy.</p>
<p>Concept 1/2. Critical junctures are a distinct kind of change that generate a distinct kind of effect. Critical junctures are rapid, discontinuous, macro-level changes. They are not wholly contingent events, unconstrained by antecedent conditions.</p>
<p>Concept 2/2. Critical junctures are changes that create a persistent effect through a self-replicating causal loop. Conceptual issues are addressed in chapters by David Collier, Rachel Beatty Riedl and Kenneth M. Roberts, and Gerardo Munck.</p>
<p>Theory 1/5. David Collier presents a CJ framework using five components: antecedent conditions, cleavage or shock, critical junctures, aftermath, and legacy. <p>Theory 2/5. Gerardo Munck revisits the theoretical foundations of CJ research. He shows how CJ research resolves two problems in the study of historical explanations: those of infinite regress and distal, nonrecurring causes. He questions some common views.</p>
<p>Theory 3/5. Munck also addresses how critical junctures themselves are explained in terms of endogenous and exogenous factors, and structure and agency. He underscores the limits of endogenous drivers of change.</p>
<p>Theory 4/5. Rachel Beatty Riedl and Kenneth M. Roberts differentiate between two types of critical juncture: generative, in which contingent choice plays a greater causal role; and activating, in which antecedent conditions play a more important causal role.</p>

<p>Methods 1/3. David Waldner challenges qualitative researchers to better address key issues of causal inference. He raises questions about the methodological role assigned to critical junctures as contingent events and holds that process tracing is a partial tool.</p>
