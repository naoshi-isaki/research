<p>The construction of the "white working class" and the myth of the "left behind" that conveniently ignores how diverse the really existing working class and left behind are and how their interests are linked has been central to the mainstreaming of far right politics</p>
<p>1/</p>
<p>It has allowed deeply racist and classist narratives to be normalized by mainstream elites who could pretend they cared about "the poor" while doing nothing to address the structural issues behind said poverty</p>
<p>"It's cultural, not economic", we're told</p>
<p>That's convenient...</p>
<p>2/<br />What's telling really is that in every country the far right has become mainstream, I can think of academics or 'intellectuals' who have legitimized these takes</p>
<p>Unsurprisingly, many of these voices have turned full reactionary by now</p>
<p>3/</p>
<p>Look at how mainstream elites loved JD Vance's Hillbilly Elegy and how they're turning against him now he's crossed a very distant line from anything decent</p>
<p>The writing was always on the wall and making the far right the voice of the left behind was always enabling it</p>
<p>4/</p>
<p>In France, we had Perrineau in the 2000s. In the UK, it was Goodwin in the mid 2010s. Many in the US post 2016 obviously hyping up Trump's working class support</p>
<p>In all cases, these theories were indulged because they provided an excuse to ignore systemic issues</p>
<p>5/</p>
<p>It speaks of the classism and demophobia core to much of our public discourse and is linked to populist hype</p>
<p>Blaming the rise of reaction on "the people" or the "left behind" (who are imagined and silent) was very convenient way to avoid looking at its mainstream roots</p>
<p>6/</p>
<p>Finally those who pretend they didn't know better were not looking really hard or casually ignoring warnings</p>
<p>In the 2000s, Mayer &amp; Collovald in France made the dangers posed by so-called populism or "Lepen-leftism" clear</p>
